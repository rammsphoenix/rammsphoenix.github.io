RAMMS Basketball Rules

Boys Kindergarten

Applies to all divisions
Specific to this division
1. Equipment & Court Setup
1.1 Hoop Height, Ball Size & Free Throw Line Division Rule

8-foot hoop  ·  25.5″ ball  ·  ~10-foot free throw line

2. Before the Game
2.1 Evaluations & Draft

All athletes must attend evaluations or make arrangements with the Director in advance. Otherwise, a coach’s child is automatically the first-round pick, and unranked athletes are randomly assigned at the end of the draft.

2.2 Coaching

The Director or League Commissioner selects coaches at their discretion, weighing volunteer service to RAMMS, past complaints, rule compliance, and RAMMS’ growth and retention goals. All coaches undergo a background check. RAMMS reserves the right to remove a coach at any time—including for noncompliance with the rules, Code of Conduct violations, or other reasons at its discretion.

2.3 Uniforms

The “home” team wears white or lighter jerseys; the “away” team wears darker jerseys.

2.4 Pre-Game Discussion

Coaches are encouraged to have a coaches’ meeting with the officials before the start of every game to address any potential issues and to work cooperatively toward having a fair, fun game for all of the athletes.

2.5 Scorekeeper & Scoreboard Operator

The home team provides an adult scorekeeper, who keeps score in the scorebook and tracks the number of periods each player plays. The visiting team provides an adult scoreboard operator/clock operator.

In the regular season only, if both head coaches agree, they may elect to use minors instead of adults as the scorekeeper and scoreboard/clock operator. Adult scorekeepers and scoreboard/clock operators are required for all playoff games.

3. Game Format
3.1 Teams

Each team will have five players on the court at one time. The teams play five-on-five.

3.2 Clock & Quarters

Each game consists of four 10-minute running quarters, with each quarter divided into two 5-minute periods. There is a 60-second official timeout at the 5-minute mark of each quarter for substitutions only.

The clock runs continuously except for timeouts; injuries; and all free throws and whistles during the last two minutes of the second half and overtime.

3.3 Timeouts

Regular season: each team has one 60-second timeout in the first half, one in the second half, and one per overtime period.

Playoffs: each team has two timeouts in the first half, two in the second half, and one per overtime period.

This is separate from the 60-second official timeout called at the 5-minute mark of each quarter for substitutions only. Possession does not change at the 5-minute timeouts, and the ball remains with the team that had possession when the timeout was called.

3.4 Change of Possession

Possession of the ball alternates at the end of each quarter, each half, and on jump balls. The team that does not win the opening jump ball is entitled to the first alternating possession.

Possession does not change at the 5-minute substitution timeouts; the ball remains with the team that had possession when the timeout was called.

4. Player Eligibility & Playing Time
4.1 Game-Day Attendance

To be eligible to play in a game, a player must be on the bench for the start of the third quarter. A player who arrives after the start of the third quarter may not play, because that player is no longer able to play the minimum number of periods (four).

4.2 Player Call-ups & Forfeit

If a team cannot field at least four of its own players within five minutes after the scheduled starting time, that team forfeits the game and the teams play a scrimmage during the time allotted.

During the regular season, a team that has four of its own players may “call up” substitute players from the league directly below their league. A team with four of its own players may call up up to two players; a team with five of its own players may call up one player.

A substitute player may be called up by a particular team only once per season. The Director of Basketball Operations or League Commissioner must approve the player to be called up before that player can play. Call-ups are only allowed during the regular season and are not allowed during the playoffs.

4.3 Substitutions

A 60-second official timeout is called at the 5-minute mark of each quarter for substitutions only. Coaching is kept to a minimum during this timeout; its purpose is to allow sufficient time to “match up” the players. Substitutions are not permitted during play at times other than the 5-minute mark and the end of each quarter, except for injury or foul disqualifications.

If one team has fewer players such that it has a higher maximum periods allowed than its opponent, the opponent with the lower maximum may designate two players to play the maximum periods allowed for its opponent, as long as everyone else on the team plays the minimum required for its number of players. For example, if Team A has 6 players (maximum 7 periods) and Team B has 8 players (maximum 6 periods), Team B may designate two players to play 7 periods as long as every other Team B player plays the minimum for an 8-player team (4 periods).

Substitution/maximum playing-time violations must be raised at the time they occur and addressed between the coaches. Coaches must track their players’ playing time. If there is a violation, the resolution is that the athlete sits out the period and does not play more than the maximum periods permitted.

4.4 Playing Time Requirements

Games consist of four 10-minute quarters, each divided into two 5-minute periods (eight periods total). For five-on-five play, the minimum and maximum periods each player must play depend on the number of players present:

9 players present: every player must play 4 periods and no player may play more than 6.
8 players present: every player must play 4 periods and no player may play more than 6.
7 players present: every player must play 4 periods and no player may play more than 6.
6 players present: every player must play 6 periods and no player may play more than 7.
5 players present: every player may play all 8 periods.

Maximum playing-time violations must be raised when they occur and addressed between the coaches, who must track their players’ playing time. If there is a violation, the athlete must sit out the period and not play more than the maximum periods permitted.

Injury and disqualification substitutions: if a player is injured, a coach may substitute a player for the injured player; if the injured player is able to return that same period, the player may return at the first available dead ball. A partial period played by an injured player counts as a full period toward that player’s maximum and minimum required play. A partial period played by a substitute counts as a full period toward the substitute’s maximum allowed periods, but does not count toward the substitute’s minimum required periods. (For example, a player required to play 4 periods who played 3 full periods plus a partial period as a substitute still needs to play one more full period; but a player with a maximum of 6 who has played 5 periods plus a partial period has used the 6th period and may not play again until overtime. A player who has reached the maximum allowed periods may not enter as a substitute.) The same treatment applies to a substitute for a fouled-out or ejected player.

If an injury or disqualification occurs early in the game, coaches are encouraged to discuss and agree on changes to the minimum/maximum playing-time requirements. In no event may a coach encourage a player to purposefully foul out or get injured to avoid maximum playing-time requirements. Playing-time requirements do not apply in overtime.

5. Defense
5.1 Defensive Style & Limitations Division Rule

Teams must play man-to-man defense. Teams may not play zone defense, double team inside or outside the paint, or defend in the backcourt except as permitted under Backcourt Defense (5.3).

Defensive players may “sag” off their man. When the offensive player is inside the three-point area, the sag should be no more than three feet; when the offensive player is outside the three-point area, the sag may be more.

Players may not steal the ball from the player they are guarding or from any other player on the court. Players may not swipe at the ball when the player they are guarding has the ball or when another player dribbles near them. Players may switch on a pick but must switch back at the first available opportunity. Players may intercept passes. Players may try to block shots when the player they are guarding shoots the ball.

5.2 Wristband Matchup System Division Rule

To help players, coaches, and referees know who is matched up to whom, all players wear a colored wristband when in the game. At the beginning of each period, one team puts on its wristbands first and the other team matches up by putting on the appropriate colored wristband.

The Director of Boys Basketball or League Commissioner provides the wristband order to the coaches at the beginning of the season. The wristband order is alphabetical by color, going from the strongest player to the weakest player. RAMMS may decide to have two wristbands of the same color for the strongest players; if so, the coaches can decide which player guards the opposing team’s player of the same wristband color.

Coaches must match their better players against the opposing team’s better players and should not try to create mismatches. If a mismatch results during play, a coach may call a timeout, tell the referees and opposing coach that they will be switching wristbands, and make the switch. If the two coaches cannot agree on the matchups, the referees decide them and report the two coaches to the Director of Basketball Operations.

Players may only guard the player who has the same colored wristband. The only exceptions are when a player is the only person available to stop a fast break, or on a switch on a pick; in those two situations the player may guard a player with a different color wristband. However, the no-double-team rules still apply.

5.3 Backcourt Defense Division Rule

Regular play: Except in the last two minutes of the second half and in overtime, a team may not play defense in the backcourt once the opposing team has gained control of the ball.

Last 2 minutes of the second half & overtime: a team may play man-to-man defense full court. All other defenses remain prohibited—double-teaming is not permitted.

5.4 Defensive Violation Enforcement

Teams are given two warnings per half with respect to each of the defensive rules set forth above. Each subsequent violation of a particular rule, per half, results in a technical foul.

6. Offense
6.1 Offensive Limitations

A team may not implement a four-corner offense or isolation offense in which the majority of the offensive players are stationed in the corners or perimeter of the court—except during the final thirty seconds of a period, when a team is holding the ball for a final shot or stalling.

Enforcement: the first violation in each half results in a warning. Each subsequent violation, per half, results in a technical foul.

7. Fouls & Free Throws
7.1 Personal Fouls

A player is disqualified from the game following a fifth personal foul.

7.2 Technical Fouls

A technical foul is charged as a personal foul and results in two points awarded to the opposing team, except in the last two minutes of the second half or in the last two minutes of overtime, when it results in a stopped clock and two free throws.

Any player, coach, or spectator receiving two technical fouls is ejected from the game and must leave the gym area. Any player, coach, or spectator who has been ejected is, at a minimum, suspended for the next game.

Technical fouls called for illegal defense do not count as personal fouls and do not count toward the two technical fouls that would lead to an ejection.

7.3 Lane Violations Division Rule

No lane (three-second) violation is enforced in this division.

7.4 Shooting Foul Penalties Division Rule

Outside the last 2 minutes: one point and the ball are awarded for fouls committed in the act of shooting (any shot).

Last 2 minutes of the second half & overtime: any defensive shooting or non-shooting foul results in a stopped clock and two free throws.

Offensive fouls result in a change of possession.

7.5 Free Throw Stepping

Players may pass over (cross) the foul line while shooting a free throw without penalty. The free throw line for the league is marked with tape prior to the game or otherwise established by the referees.

8. Overtime
8.1 Regular Season

If the score is tied at the end of regulation, the teams play a two-minute overtime period. The rules applying to the last two minutes of the second half apply to the last two minutes of overtime. Playing-time requirements do not apply in overtime, and each coach may put whatever five players they want on the floor. If the game remains tied after the two-minute overtime, it is scored as a tie.

8.2 Playoffs

In the playoffs, the overtime period is five minutes. If the game remains tied after a five-minute overtime, the teams play additional two-minute overtime periods until the result is no longer a tie. Playing-time requirements do not apply in overtime, and each coach may put whatever five players they want on the floor.

9. Playoffs
9.1 Tiebreakers

Playoff rankings are based on winning percentage. In the event of a tie when determining playoff rankings, the following tiebreakers are used in order: (1) head-to-head; (2) record vs. common opponents; (3) coin flip (or random number generator if more than two teams remain tied at that point).

9.2 Call-up Restrictions

Player call-ups are not permitted in the playoffs. See Section 4.2.

10. Safety & Conduct
10.1 Safety Requirements

Basketball shoes must be worn on the court. No watches, necklaces, or personal jewelry may be worn during play. No casts, whether soft or hard, are permitted under any circumstances. No spectators are allowed on the court during the game.

10.2 Ejections

Any player, coach, or spectator ejected from the game by the referee, for whatever reason, must immediately leave the area. Any ejected player, coach, or spectator is automatically suspended for the next game, regardless of whether it is the regular season or postseason. All ejections are reviewed by the RAMMS D&R Committee for possible Code of Conduct violations and, if warranted, further disciplinary action.

10.3 Games Are Final

RAMMS will not change the outcome of a game once it has been called final by the official. Coaches should raise issues with the official when they occur. The RAMMS board reserves the right to forfeit any team for willful noncompliance with the rules or other misconduct.

10.4 Coaching Requirements

A RAMMS-approved Head or Assistant Coach must be present at every practice, game, or team event. If a coach is unable to make a scheduled game, contact the Director of Boys or Girls Basketball or your league Commissioner to discuss potential solutions.

10.5 Governing Rules Reference

The current National Federation Basketball Rules Book applies in all situations not specifically addressed by RAMMS rules.
